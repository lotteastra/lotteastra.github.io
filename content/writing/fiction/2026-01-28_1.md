The warnings. It kept on shouting at me. Overspeed. Overspeed. Overspeed. I started to move sluggish. My imagined metal body not floating in the air like it did. I kept on pressing the throttle further. 

I was a missile. I was a shell. I was the cannon. I was the propellant. I was the gunpowder. I was the shell casing.

Paved in twisted barrels, dud shells and rocket shrapnel. 

It was just a little further now. It was like being in pursuit of a meal, the way the smell surrounded me now. I was so hungry. Biological imperatives to kill. A beast doesn’t make paths. A beast doesn’t try to right the wrongs left behind. There is only one road open to such a beast. It was above. It was to follow the smell of the prey. The darkened sky. 

The last line of defenses were light. There was little left to do. Drones couldn’t reach back here enough. This defensive fortification had extracted all the resources it could, there was little more to be gained. I reached into the sky. I gripped the cables and tore them apart. Immediate pleasure filling every single nodule in my brain. Ripping. Ripping. Ripping. The sky now shone out all the clearer. I couldn’t feel the sun’s warmth. Not anymore. That didn’t matter. I felt so good all the same. With the drones cut friendly forces could advance, but that left the defenses. There was a chorus of artillery still firing behind the bunker, rendering death kilometers away. 

The concrete was easy to break, it felt so good. The rubble fell apart. The marks in it were jagged, not made by a human, not made by a machine, they were made by an animal. Desperate and without pattern except the pattern of random motions that marked a predator gorging itself on still warm meat. The core was covered by the collapse. The feeling was intense. A mind that was now so immersed in happy chemicals that rational thought would not be able to continue. Washed away in bliss. The one who conquered the hill standing there idle hands still buried inside the rubble.

There was something and it was touching me. I opened my eyes. The bright lights filling me too. My eyes hurt. A dull ache. It wasn't the thing that served any function to code. It was a reminder of the reality of the body. The body that was mine.

Clark stood over me. I could recognize the face. One of the few faces I could recognize. My own mother's face left in a dull haze. Somewhere warm and green. Somewhere long ago. 

"He's up."

I'm up Clark. Wait. Wait no he can't. He's not reacting to my thoughts right away. These thoughts are my thoughts. I couldn't will my mouth into moving. Tongue locked behind a ventalator. The doctor began to remove the breathing tube from my still stupefied body. Laying me to rest on that table. My eyes danced trying to catch a better look around. At the dull lighting that stared into me. At Clark. At the doctor, the white coat fussing over me. 

"He'll be up soon. The last of the paralytics are just leaving his system." I could hear the doctor speak. I could hear the way the sound bounced around the room before reaching me.

"Took you guys a while."

"It's always like this. Cold storage. It takes a moment for the kidneys to start working again. The liver."

"Right. Right, so this one is from cold storage directly?"

"Not a very popular model. I mean, would you pick it?"

"He is up."

"It's short. The hair is a little long. It's not repulsive. If anything it's cute. It's not. What one would associate with a battle hardened veteran though. Not really going to go down well with the ladies."

"That's my Whiskey."

"I suppose there's no accounting for taste."

There wasn't much to care about. I didn't prefer this body much at all either. Still. It is better to have a body than not. To have one.

"How's the datalink on your end." The doctor turned to Clark.

"It's working."

"I'm going to make my rounds. Call me if anything starts to beep."

"Right doc." 

I looked over. I could see Clark at out of the corner of my eye. He was tapping his foot. I was keeping him wait. There was no good in that. He moved in his chair, back and forth. Like some infernal strings were being pulled. He was reacting to something. It wouldn't do. Leaving him alone like this. I started to exert more will into the force of locomotion. Willing my mouth into motion.

"Clark." I tried to say. My words were slurred. My voice breathless. The sound coming out of me like a prayer. 

"Whiskey, how are you?"

"I'm okay. I'm okay Clark."

"I know you're okay. There's nothing beeping over there."

"Well."

I coughed. Spewing over myself. My breathing and the monitors hitching for a second. Then I started to choke. My tongue was still numb still not responding. I tried to roll over onto my side. A hand did it. 

Someone else's hand. Someone else's hand on my own body. It was warm. It was so warm. Against the cold sheet of metal that I had been laid to rest on.

"Easy, easy." He said. Rolling me on my side. Letting the spit run and dribble.

I could feel the wires strain. I could feel the tubes stitched into me pulling tight. Pulling into me. My connection. The coughing stopped. An involuntary physical action all throughout me. Even that sensation filled me. I drank greedily of it. Then he set me down. He set me down with the care a parent would show. The fact he had done this before written into his movements. 

He stepped into the hallway. Distant voice calling for the doctor to return. Footsteps ringing through the white hallway. Heralding the arrival.

"Coughing? Not uncommon, almost back is he? Let's try and get him to sit up."

The doctor held me. Soft and firm. I augmented his movement with my own. Helping him help me to sit up. I could maintain the position and he let go, hands free, like a sculptor admiring his work. Admiring the life he had sculpted into bone and flesh. 

"Let's get him out of all that." Clark gestured towards the monitors and pipes. 

"Thank you." I said. Tongue now more under control. 

"We've got an appointment to keep." Clark said. "We need to get to the mission. That's all."

Words sever and cut. Just as the tubes were removed. The doctor handed me cotton and held it over as the canula was removed. It stung. I noticed now more sensations. The taste. The taste of rubber so full and complete in my mouth. Unpleasant. Unremitting. 

"Do you have anything? Anything for the taste?"

The doctor turned to Clark. He let out a sigh and produced some freeze dried lemon slices. Handed them to me. I held out my hand to recieve the bounty. Another sensation, dripping into me. The sour, the sweet, dissolving into me. I liked it. Chasing away the taste of rubber. 

The doctor left confident that I was all together. Clark started to fiddle with a bag he had with him, started to produce clothing. He was wearing a suit so it was natural that so should I. Something so stylish it had to be borrowed. Not the usual grey overalls I remembered seeing him in. His hands were wet. His face sweaty. He removed the robe from me. Exposing my pale skin. My body. Every part of it. It was a complete body that was for certain. Purpose unknown. 

Not a question that would be asked of a regular body, but one that had been created of deliberate action begs a question. I could move but my movements were sluggish. Clark's soft, wet, warm, hands touching me and adorning me. Baptizing me in the cloth of civilization before patting me on the back.

"It's time to go." 

He held my hand. I tried to meet him. Trying to will my legs. Slow and steady. They moved. The first steps of a newborn body. The sound betraying the disorder in the gait. Step. Step step. Step. Clark there to steady me. As the drugs cleared out. The beat went on. 

We left the hospital behind. I saw the sky. It was blue. It was the same blue of the battle. Behind fibre optic cables. Up and above. Not a cloud in sight. I could feel the sun. The real sun touching my hand too. I could feel it now. A smile spreading across my face. Muscles moving in response to stimuli. 

There was a car waiting for us already. It smelled nicer than the antisceptic stained floors of before. Through black tinted windows I tried to look around me but Clark poked me stealing my attention away.

"I know it's nice to be out here and all, but we have a job to do."

"I know, we're meeting a sponsor of ours right?"

"Right. This guy, he's a general, that's his relation to us. He does other things. Those other things are how he got to where he is. He's a strange guy. I had hoped we wouldn't have his attention but here we are."

"What do you mean, strange guy? What do you mean exactly?"

"He should be in there." He tapped my head. "Try to think of General Fullbright, he should be there." 

I tried to feel around for it. Grabbing at something. It was there it was almost there. General Fullbright. I repeated it.

"They didn't even do that right." Clark said with a sigh.

"I'm sorry." 

"It's not really your fault. I'll just give you the run down. He's a General, he's our boss. He's a businessman, that's how he got to be a general. He bought his way in. He was a war profiteer. That's not even his main moneymaker these days though. He helps run a betting market. Trading stocks and shares, except they're people's lives. Ups and downs. This will happen and I'll bet you 10 million, pocket change."

"He seems like a very distinguished guy Clark."

"I sometimes forget your babyfood mind." He touched me as he said that, touching my head. "That man's biography, he's a con man. He's a con man through and through. The worst kind of con man. The kind of con man who thinks that the luck he has is his own."

I wanted to ask Clark. What I knew about him. What Vulture had told me. I wanted to ask Clark. Was he not just the very same guy. I looked out the window. Saw the sun. There was some greenery here, regimented but withering. In small patches as designated. There were roads. Cars. Robots darting around. I saw no people. Wait. One on the left in a white jumpsuit. Head to toe. I saw him. The light shining through the grey white towers. Windows poking through them. 

"Did you get any of that?"

"Con man. Gambling app."

"You two should be buddies, you gamble every day of your life."

"It's not gambling. If you don't have a choice."

"You do have a choice. You could hang back. Would be worse for me you know, better for you, get some steady kills rolling from the back line."

"Does it matter."

"Yeah? This is living and dying. This is the most anything could matter."

"Why? It's just death."

He looked at me with eyes that stabbed into me. Then his expression softened. The resolve leaving him.

"Yeah. You're right. It is just death. At the end of the day. That's all this is."

A question kept on bubbling up from my belly. My real belly. Up my real feeding tube. Flesh and blood. Begging me to spill my guts.

"I forget sometimes." Clark said looking out the window. "How it feels, why it matters."

"Does it need to. I just do what I have to."

"You don't enjoy it? Fighting? The battles? Even that little kiddie pool game of yours?"

"I don't know. I didn't get a choice."

"Yeah. I know the feeling." He said again looking out the window. "Don't go dying on me." 

"I won't." 

"I know you're going to die. The way you fight. You're going to die. Something somewhere. There's already a bullet out there. It has your name on it. Until the date and the place you meet it. Just keep on raking it in for me. For the time being."

"You'll find someone else."

"I will. Will be a pain in the ass but. I will."

The city rolled by. Past big tower blocks and winding into smaller roads like tributaries. I noticed now. The wall. At the end of the street, hanging over the houses. Grey concrete stretching until it touched the blue sky.
