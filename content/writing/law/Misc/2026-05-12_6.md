title: Judicial Review Illegality
---

One of the three major grounds of review and therefore one of the most confusing cases. Simple/Narrow Vires, Broad Vires, Abandonment of Discretion, and Abuse of Discretion. The argument is that a public body simply just acted illegally. 

The decisionmaker must underestand correctly the law that regulates his decision making power and give effect to it. Lord Diplock GCHQ

Where does the power originate is the first concern. What is the content, purpose and scope? Lack/absence of power to act, substantive or implied. Parliament's intention crucial, what powers did Parliament actually confer. 

They did not have the power to do what they did. Narrow Vires. 

Fewings 1995 3 All ER 20. A local government authority prohibited hunting stags on the land the council had, the statute was related to acquisition but they did not actually have that power in statute, so the court said that the particular statutory power they were relying on did not allow them to act as they did. 

Hazell v. Hammersmith and Fulham LBC [1992] AC 1. A slightly more complicated case. The council engaged in interest swap agreements. The legal power they relied on was for borrowing and not debt management. So they did not have the power to do what they were trying to do. 

There is leeway for interpretation. 

Broad vires is misuse of power. They have the power but they did not use it appropriately. 

Look at power and where the power originates. Most public decision powers are in statute but some are common law. You examine the four corners of the statutes, if they acted outside then they were acting illegally. There are explicit limitations. Those are easy to identify. Look at the ordinary English meaning of the words of the statute. 

Pepper v. Hart [1993] AC 593. You cannot look behind a statute but you can use the Hansard to understand the statute. Find out if a decisionmaker went beyond their powers. 

There are also implicit limitations. 

Principle of legality, laws are supposed to uphold peoples rights. Simms [2000] 2 AC 115. Sound economic management, Bromley LBC v. GLC [1983] 1 AC 768. That was the implicit limitation on the statutory power that was argued successfully and found in that case by the House of Lords. Talked about running a loss. 

Errors of law and interpretation. Anisminic [1969] 2 AC 147, the power exists but the public body has misused it. There was a British company that owned millions of pounds of assets in Egypt that were repossessed, they want compensation and in contradiction of the law despite an ouster clause. 

Acting in Breach of HRA S.6

Error of fact can also be analyzed. E v. SoSHD [2004] EWCA Civ 49. Illegality and misinterpretation of the law. 

Abandonment of Discretion

A lot of decisions are discretionary. Abandonment means that the power is not even used in the first place. 

Improper Delegation I

A discretionary power may not be delegated even further. 

Barnard v. National Dock Labour Board [1953] 2 QB 18. The body responsible for the ports and managing them, delegated powers further than they were supposed to. 

Caltona Doctrine emerges from Caltona ltd. v. Commissiners of Works, if a power is delegated to a minister they may be delegated to a civil servant but the minister is still responsible. 

British Oxygen co. v. Minister of Technology [1977] AC 610. Fettering discretion. Policies to guide discretion are allowed but cannot be completely closed to any exceptions. The case where there were these gas bottles that cost a little bit under the discretionary policy for these cylanders that were below the limit. Argued that the board of trade that they could not claim. The court says that you can have a general policy but you must be willing to depart from it in some cases. You should not close your mind. Fettering discretion.
