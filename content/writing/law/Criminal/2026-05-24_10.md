title: Theft
---

Found in the Theft Act 1968 Section 1

Made of 5 elements 

1. Appropriation of

2. Property

3. Belonging to another

4. Dishonestly

5. With intention to permanently deprive

AR: Appropriation of property belonging to another

MR: Dishonestly with intention to permanently deprive

PROPERTY

S.4(1) Defines property as 'money and all other prorperty real or personal including things in action and other intangible property. 

Debt counts as 'a thing in action,' 

Land cannot be stolen. Though if you are a trustee and sell more land than you have then that can be theft.

As for things on the land it falls into two categories 

S.4(2) theft act states 

When he is trustee or personal representative or is authorized by power of attorney or as liquidator of a company or otherwise to sell or dispose of land belonging to another and he appropriates the land or anything forming part of it by dealing with it in breach of the confidence reposed in him. 

Those in possession of the land do not commit theft for removing something from the land. 

THose who are not in posession may be included by S.4(2)(b) and can be found guilty of theft of things forming part of the land when they are not in posession of it. Or causing something to be severed. 

Tenants in posession would not be guilty of removing something from the land they were renting unless it was a 'fixture or structure. Following Theft Act S.4(2)(c). 

For fruit and plants it is covered by S>4(3) which states that it is not theft to pick flowers or fruit from wild land unless they do it for reward or sale or other commercial purposes. 

R v. Gimbert -> Mentally disabled V was pressured by D into signing over a house. Found not guilty since land cannot be stolen but this would be dealt with by fraud nowadays. 

S.4(4) deals with wild animals. 

Oxford v. Moss -> Information cannot be stolen and would be dealt with civily or through other offences like computer misuse. 

Sharpe -> Bodies are generally not property however,

If a cropse is reduced to another's posession it may become property. So if a hospital has taken a body into its possession then it becomes property. It can be stolen. 

Welsh -> Bodily products such as blood or urine can however be stolen. 

BELONGING TO ANOTHER

Marshall -> Generally the law of property will be used to ascertain if property belongs to another.

S.5(1) Theft act defines that property shall be regarded as belonging to any person having possession in control of it or having in it any proprietary right or interest. 

Hibbert v. McKiernan -> Collecting golf balls may not be abandoned as the golf balls may belong to the golf course themselves as they have 'possession and control,' over the ball. 

Williams v. Phillips -> Property in a bin still belongs to the person who put it there and on collection possession passes to the local authority. At no point has the property been abandoned. 

R (Ricketts) v. Basildon Magistrates Court -> Held that there was theft where D appropriated items from a charity bin behind a charity shop. They were in the possession or control of the shop and therefore the conviction should stand. 

Kelly -> You can steal from someone who originally stole the property, that they had no right to possess it does not act as a defense. 

Turner (no. 2) -> You can also steal from yourself, this is where D took D's own car from the garage without paying the bill and D was convicted of theft even though the property stolen was his own car, he had relinquished it to the car repair shop and convicted of theft since they had control over it lawfully. 

S.5(2) Property belonging to a trust will belong to all trustees. 

S.5(3) Property where it is recieved under an expectation to be used a certain specific way may be stolen if not used for that purpose. 

R v. Hall -> Travel agent took money as deposits for flights to the US. He paid the money into the firm's general account. None of the flights materialized and he did not refund and it was argueed that the money did not belong to another. The court found for the travel agent arguing that the customers did not hand over the money expecting that the particular money they had paid would be used for that purpose. Therefore while they were entitled to civil remedies it was not theft. 

Klineberg and Marsden -> Theft was found in that case where timeshares were being sold with their being built at a later date but the money given was never deposited into the account to be held while construction was ongoing and instead was used for other things. Therefore they were guilty of theft. 

Wain -> Guilty of theft when D took money that was supposed to be paid to a charity and did not do so. 

S.5(4) Where you are given property by mistake you are obligated to return it and it is considered to be belonging to another. 

AG's Ref No.1 of 1983 -> Police officer was overpaid their salary and was under a legal obligation to return it once they became aware of the mistake. 

APPROPRIATES 

S.3(1) 'Any assumption by a person of the rights of an owner amounts to an appropriation even when done innocently or without stealing. The assumption of ownership rights is sufficient. 

Morris -> D appropriated items by switching the sticky labels indicating price at a shop. That amounted to apropraition. 

Pitham and Hehl -> Selling will amount to acting as an owner. 

Briggs -> Making someone deal with the property a certain way will not amount to appropriation. 

R v. Hinks -> Even if property was handed over as a valid gift it could be an appropriation in criminal terms. Case of the woman with a man of 'limited intelligence,' who withdrew money from his bank every day and handed the money to her. Hinks was convicted of theft. Equally it was found to be dishonest

R v. Hinks dissent -> However argued that in civil law it was a valid gift and therefore that should not have been an issue but again this is a dissent with no legal force. 

R v. Gomez -> Manager of a shop, a customer who was an acquaintance offered two stolen cheques as payment for goods. Gomez was aware that the cheques were stolen but lied to his manager and persuaded them to accept the cheques saying that they were as good as cash. It was argued that because the manager had consented to the transfer then there had been no appropriation. 

R v. Lawrence -> Taxi driver case, held that even with consent it could be appropriation. Where a student was massively overcharged for a taxi. 

R v. Darroux -> Manager of a care home owned by a charity. Submitted forms claiming overtime pay to a firm that controlled the charity's accounts. The money was paid directly. She was charged with theft but the question was if she had appropriated the money in the first place. Found to be fraud by misrepresentation and not theft. The bank account is a legal claim. D had not done anything to the bank account, they had submitted false invoices to persuade a transfer. However they did have an obligation under S.5(4) to return the money and any subsequent dealings with the money could amount to an appropriation but not discussed in that case. 

Broom v. Crowther -> D found out that an item was stolen and reported it, therefore he did not appropriate it. 

However S.3(1) Keeping as an owner could be done by omission in theory but no cases on it. 

Atakpu -> D hired a car in Germany and decided to steal it driving it all the way to Dover. CA held that the Ds had not committed the theft in England, they had committed it the moment they decided to permanently deprive the owner of the car somewhere in Germany or Belgium but once they had stolen the car they were not continuing to steal it wherever they drove it. 

S.3(2) It is not theft to buy something as a bona fide purchasor for value without knowing that it is stolen. They do not become a thief upon knowing it. However this does not apply for gifts. May be guilty of handling stolen goods however. 

INTENTION TO PERMANENTLY DEPRIVE 

Velumyl -> Intention to permanently deprive found even when aiming to replace the items taken with the same items or items of the same value. However this is relevant when looking into dishonesty. 

Easom -> D picked up a handbag and intended to steal any valuable contents he found but he found no valuable contents. He was acquitted since he was convicted of stealing the specific items that he did not steal however if worded such that it was theft of the contents of the bag then he could have been convicted potentially. 

S.6(1) Provides a definition for intending to permanently deprive. A person appropriating proeprty belogning to another without meaning the other permanently to lose the very thing itself is to be regarded as having the intention to permanently deprive. Including disposal or lending.

S6(2) Where a person having posession or control lawfully or not of property belonging to another parts with this proeprty under a condition as to its return which he may not be able to perform this if done for purposes of his own and without the other's authority amounts to treating the proeprty as his own to dispose of regardless of the other's rights. 

Mitchell -> Disposal requires concealment and therefore will not apply where the property is in the clear view of D. 

Raphael -> Selling the item back to V also amounts to intention to permanently deprive. 

DPP v. Lavender -> Moving a council door from one place to another does not amount to intention to permanently deprive actually. 

DPP v. SJ, PI, RC -> Where Ds took V's headphones smashed them and returned them it was held to be theft as breaking them was acting as an owner and therefore theft. 

Fernandez -> Using the property in risky investments will also be sufficient to be intention to permanently deprive. 

R v. Marshall -> The TFL tickets case. They were convicted of stealing but tried to appeal on the basis that they did not intend to permanently deprive London Underground of the tickets. Conviction found on the basis that the tickets were a legal right between only the purchasor and London Underground and therefore could not be sold onto someone else. 

DISHONESTY 

S.2(1)(a-c) Someone is not dishonest if they (a) believes they have the legal right to deprive someone of it (b) if he appropriated it believing consent (c) if he believes that the person from whom he is taking the property cannot be found by taking reasonable steps.

S.2(2) Someone is still dishonest even if they would have been willing to pay for what they stole. 

Small -> This belief in legality need not be reasonable or based on how the law factually is, merely that D believed the law to be so. 

Skivington -> Held up a wage clerk demanding his wages, no theft or robbery because D believed himself entitled to those wages. Not dishonest. 

Common law test for dishonest comes from IVEY

Ivey v. Genting Casinos -> Case where a blackjack player won big time by using minute differences in his odds to make bets and win. The casino tried to avoid paying out. Displaced the Ghosh test and replaced it with the Ivey test which has two elements. (1) What did D believe was the case, example of someone who comes from a country where public transit is free believed it to be free and did not pay was not dishonest. (2) Would a reasonable person believe that D was acting dishonestly based on the facts as D saw them. 

R v. Barton and Booth -> Barton owned a care home in Lancashire and groomed wealthy residents into giving him money or property in their wills. The judge decided to use the Ivey test for dishonesty. Was D's conduct dishonest by the standards of an ordinary decent person bearing in mind the facts as D believed them to be. 

THEFT THEORY

Debate surrounding Gomez where it was found that even an action consented to by a victim may still be a crime. 

S Gardner 1993 SCHOLARLY ARGUMENT -> Consent can be problematic in cases where the victim is confused either unintentionally or indeed wholly intentionally, the law shifting to protect those victims is important and therefore the reasoning in Gomez is desireable because it protects the vulnerable. 

Giles and Uglow 1992 SCHOLARLY ARGUMENT -> However, Gomez could make theft a mind crime, where merely touching an article with the correct mens rea could constitute fraud no matter what. Gomez unsatisfactorially widens the scope of theft. 

Melissaris 2007 SCHOLARLY ARGUMENT -> Appropriation is essentially a mental attitude but not a harmful enough attitude until it manifests in an action. Arguing in favour of it being a mind crime basically.

DISHONESTY

Finch 2021 STUDY -> Found that 57-43 public did not find it dishonest for a carer to request something from the will of a dying person. Therefore even though Ivey relies on a 'reasonable person test,' it is weak in the sense that what is and isn't dishonest is variable from person to person. 

Green 2014 -> Sometimes theft offences are necessary to remedy an unjust social order and therefore sometimes it could be said that D is the real victim and not V.
