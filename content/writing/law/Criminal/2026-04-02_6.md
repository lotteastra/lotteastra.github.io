SELF DEFENSE

Two elements

1. D was or believed they were facint a threat from V
2. D used a level of force against the threat or threat as it was believed to be was reasonable in the circumstances

This defense comes from common law but also S. 3 of the Criminal Law Act 1967, though the legal rules are seen to be the same regardless of the source. S. 3 does specifically pertain to the prevention of a crime or assisting in lawful arrest. 

DPP v. Bayer, Morris and Oraki v. CPS -> This it can be used in relation to any offence but it is only available where D uses force. 

Riddell -> Self-defense is only avaialble where Davis LJ 'where a person uses force in order to meet actual or percieved force or threat of force.' 

However!

Oraki v. CPS -> Obiter, it did not need to be shown that D was using force. 

S. 76 of the Criminal Justice and Immigration Act 2008 tried to clarify. 

Keane -> CA case said that the elements are as follows

1. The victim must pose a threat
2. The threat must be unjustified
3. The use of force must be reasonable
4. The amount of force used must be reasonable 
5. D must be acting to in order to defend themselves or another or property

VICTIM MUST POSE A THREAT

R v. Hitchens -> H was charged with an assault on B. His defense was that he acted in self-defense. He assaulted her in order to prevent her allowing a man into her flat who H believed would assault. He was charged with assault at the first instance but the CA allowed an appeal. The discussion was brief and muddied the waters between duress and self defense.

R v. Williams (Demario) -> The defence could apply in cases where D was attempting to stop V from taking their property. It could not be used if V had stolen the property and D was using force to recovery. 

THE THREAT MUST BE UNJUSTIFIED

Jones -> Crime committed in an attempt to stop the Iraq war could not be justified on the basis on self defense because going to war in Iraq was not an offence. 

THE USE OF FORCE MUST BE REASONABLE

R v. Martin (Anthony) -> D shot two men who entered his house at night. D was convicted of murder and on appeal there was an attempt to introduce new evidence to argue that D was suffering from DR and he was more likely to believe he was about to be attacked than an ordinary person would be. Householder part of S. 76 of the 2008 Act, if they could have escaped then use of force will be held to be unreasonable. 

S. 76(6A) -> There is no duty to retreat 

Bird -> D tried to unsuccessfuly escape and then used force to attack, then the jury is likely to think D acted reasonably. 

Law does allow a pre-emptive strike -> Beckford

So long ad the pre-emptive strike is reasonable -> AG's Ref No. 2 of 1983, prepared petrol bombs during widespread rioting. 

The attack also needn't be imminent or immediate -> AG for NI's Ref. No. 1 1975

THE AMOUNT OF FORCE USED MUST BE REASONABLE 

Yaman -> Amount of force must be reasonable

Oye -> COnfirmed long line of cases is that the question is if a reasonable person would say that the level of force was reasonable and not D. 

AG for NI's Ref. No. 1 1975 -> These circumstances of proportionality need to be seen in the context that you're in the controlled environment of the court room evaluating it. 

S. 76 (7)(a) -> D was acting in the agony of the moment
