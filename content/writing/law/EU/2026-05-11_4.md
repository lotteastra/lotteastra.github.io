---
title: EU Law Direct Effect Preliminary Ruling Proceedure
---

Designed to ensure uniformity in the interpretation of EU law to make it effective. It would endanger the primacy of EU law if it was uniform, and it would also prevent the effectiveness. They need to apply across the entire EU. It is a decentralized model because national courts may apply EU law directly. Designed so that EU law is interpreted the same by all national courts. 

Cases where EU law is relevant may emerge in national courts. That directive should be interpreted the same across all states, and even though they're transposed they might still require EU interpretation. VDE and HDE might require EU law to become relevant and applicable. Preliminary Ruling Proceedure exists to this end. Uniform appliccation and interpretation of EU law. 

A national court asks questions to the ECJ about how the law in question should apply. The ECJ gives an authoritative interpretation and then if a decision based on this interpretation is valid or invalid. A key point of this, is that it is a process that is a dialogue between a national court and the ECJ. These references come from national courts. The parties cannot ask, it is just the national court. The court is not obliged to refer regardless of what the parties say. 

Private individuals have raised issues of EU law to delay a ruling, and that is why it is just for the courts to decide. Legal certainty and the rule of law is more important and a vital part of the preliminary ruling proceedure. Not every time does a case need to be referred to the ECJ, they have the power but no obligation under 267(2) TFEU and is discretionary. However 267(3) TFEU there is an obligation to refer. Paragraph 3 creates a duty to refer to the ECJ is what creates uniformity. Courts who decision there is no judicial remedy against have a duty to refer. When the issue goes to appeal, or if there is no higher court, then the case must be referred to the ECJ. 

Arbitrartion is not sufficient it must pass through a court. There is an obligation to refer by lower courts in some circumstances. When the issue touches on the validity of EU law, and this comes from the case of FOTO FROST, it was a trade case and there was a decision by the commission that was argued against an EU regulation. The German court could not decide because it was about the hierarchy of EU law and the validity of an EU decision, and they went to the ECJ, and they had to make a reference. Only an EU Court can decide on the validity of EU law. They have an obligation to refer. 

There are three exceptions found by the case law CILFIT. 

1. Where the ECJ has already issued an interpretation or a judgement then they do not have to refer again, just follow the ruling. Act Eclaire.

2. The EU law should be clear and unambigious in a prior ruling. It must be valid to all member states in all language. 

Kobler -> case where the SC in Italy dealt with an EU law issue and they talked about how it was Act Eclaire and therefore did not refer, but they thought that there was a need to refer. This is a violation of EU law, which creates damages to an individual then that individual may go under state liability for damages to the ECJ. 

3. One last exception where the interpretation of EU law was not relevant to the case. CIM case against Italy, where that case reached the Italian SC and they went to the ECJ and the ECJ said it was not relevant to the outcome, so there is no obligation or need to refer even when there is no higher court. 

It is not an appeal mechanism. The ECJ does not review the case from scratch, they just interpret the point of law, and do not go to the facts, they just interpret what the EU law says with reference to the facts. The case is suspended while it is in the ECJ, and then the national court applies the interpretation when it gets that interpretation back. 

Case of Foglia -> If the EU law point is hypothetical then they will not bother. If the EU law point is not relevant to the decision. If the question is not relevant to the law. If the question is too unclear and difficult for the ECJ to interpret. 

The case then bounces back and they have to take the ECJ interpretation, and it also applies retroactively since it merely restates what EU law always was. Indirectly all courts across all MS are bound by any ECJ decision. 

Article 158 EU Withdrawal Agreement may after 8 years after national courts can make references to the ECJ about issues concerning free movement under the WA. 

Exists to ensure EU law is interpreted under Article 19 TFEU.
